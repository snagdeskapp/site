Practical completion, often shortened to PC, is one of the most consequential moments in a construction contract. It is not the same as "everything is perfect". It means the building is fit for its intended purpose and can be occupied, with any remaining minor items recorded to be put right.

## Why it matters

Reaching practical completion typically triggers several things at once:

- **Possession passes to the client**, who can occupy and insure the building.
- **The defects liability period starts**, during which the contractor returns to correct recorded items.
- **Retention may be partly released**, depending on the contract.
- **Liability for damage and insurance responsibility shifts**, again depending on the contract.

Because it moves responsibility and starts a clock, the snag list attached to practical completion carries real weight. Items recorded at that point are clearly within scope. Items noticed much later can be harder to raise.

## What is normally required

The specifics depend on the contract, but generally the works must be finished apart from minor items, statutory approvals and certificates must be in place, and the building must be safe to occupy. Outstanding items are listed rather than ignored.

This is why a photographed, located and numbered list matters so much at this stage: it is the record both sides refer back to.
