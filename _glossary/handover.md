Handover is the event, practical completion is the contractual status that usually accompanies it. In everyday site language the two are used almost interchangeably, but they are not identical: practical completion is a decision about the state of the works, and handover is the act of giving the building over.

## What normally changes hands

- **Keys and access**, including any access control or alarm codes
- **The snag list** of outstanding items, with an agreed programme for closing them
- **Certificates and approvals**, such as electrical, gas and building control documentation
- **Operating information**, often bundled as an [O&M manual](/glossary/o-and-m-manual/)
- **Warranties** for installed equipment and finishes

## Why the snag list is the centrepiece

Everything else on that list is largely administrative. The snag list is the part both sides will refer back to, because it defines what is still owed. A vague list creates weeks of argument. A numbered, photographed, located list closes items in visits rather than in emails.

That is why the walk immediately before handover matters so much, and why producing the report the same day, rather than days later, changes how quickly a job actually finishes.
