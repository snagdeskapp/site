The word is used across UK and Irish construction for anything from a paint run to a door that will not close properly. It usually implies something minor and correctable rather than a structural failure, though the term is applied loosely and the contract, not the vocabulary, decides what has to be fixed.

## What is typically recorded as a snag

- Finish defects such as poor paint coverage, visible joints or uneven plaster
- Fit and alignment problems, for example doors catching or cupboards out of line
- Broken or missing sealant and mastic
- Services fitted crooked or proud, such as sockets, switches and alarms
- Damage caused during the works
- Work simply not completed, such as a missing trim or vent

What is not normally a snag is a design decision you dislike, or ordinary wear that appears after occupation.

## How a snag should be recorded

For the item to be actionable, it needs a photograph that clearly shows the defect, a location precise enough to walk to, and a short factual description. Marking up the photograph, by drawing a box or circle on the exact area, is what removes most disputes.

A collection of snags is a **snag list**, and the document produced from it is a **snagging report**. In the United States the same thing is called a punch list.
