Most snagging reports fail for the same reason: they describe a problem clearly enough for the person who wrote them, and not clearly enough for anyone else. The builder receives forty lines, cannot tell what half of them refer to, fixes the obvious ones and pushes back on the rest. Two more visits follow.

A report that gets actioned is not longer or more formal. It is more specific.

## Structure it so it can be worked through

**Number every item.** This sounds trivial and it is the single most useful thing you can do. Numbered items can be referred to on the phone, ticked off, and disputed individually rather than as a block.

**Group by location.** A trade sent to plot 14 wants everything for plot 14 in one place. A flat list of items scattered across a building creates unnecessary walking and missed items. Group by plot, then by room.

**Keep one snag per entry.** "Kitchen needs attention, sealant and worktop chip and cupboard door" is three items pretending to be one. They get partially fixed and then argued about.

## Write each entry properly

Each snag needs three components.

**The photograph.** One clear image of the defect. If the defect is small or the room is busy, mark up the image: draw a box or circle around the exact area. This removes nearly all the ambiguity that causes return visits.

**The location.** Precise enough to walk to without asking. "Bedroom 2, window cill, left return" not "upstairs bedroom". On a multi-plot site, include the plot.

**The description.** Short, factual, and about the defect rather than your feelings about it.

> Weak: "Bad sealant in bathroom, very poor."
>
> Strong: "Silicone bead to bath-to-tile junction is broken over approximately 200mm on the tap end."

## Describe the defect, not the fix

Unless a specific method was contractually specified, say what is wrong and let the trade decide how to correct it. "Re-plaster the whole wall" invites an argument about scope. "Visible crack approximately 300mm long above the door head, running to the corner" states the fact and leaves the remedy where it belongs.

The exception is where a standard or a specification applies. Then cite it, because that is not your opinion, it is the agreed requirement.

## Common mistakes that get reports ignored

- **No photographs.** A written-only list is the easiest thing in the world to dispute.
- **Vague locations.** "Hallway" in a building with four hallways.
- **Emotional language.** It reads as a complaint rather than a record, and it invites a defensive response.
- **Mixed-up scope.** Recording design choices or ordinary wear alongside genuine defects damages the credibility of the whole list.
- **Sending it days later.** Momentum matters. A report that lands the same day gets worked on while the site team is still on the job.

## Make it look like a professional document

Presentation is not vanity here. A report with your company name, a logo, a date, numbered items and photographs signals that the inspection was methodical, and it gets treated accordingly. The same list sent as a WhatsApp thread does not.
