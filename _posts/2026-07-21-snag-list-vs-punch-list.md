They are the same document. A **snag list** and a **punch list** both mean a record of outstanding items that need putting right before a job is accepted as complete. The difference is geography, not meaning.

- **Snag list** is standard in the UK and Ireland. The verb is "snagging", and an individual item is "a snag".
- **Punch list** is standard in the United States. The phrase comes from the old practice of punching a hole in a paper list beside each item as it was completed.

## Why you might meet both in the UK

On a domestic UK build you will almost only ever hear "snagging". Where the terms collide is on commercial work: an international client, a US-headquartered tenant fitting out an office, or a contract written to an American standard will often use "punch list" throughout, including in the documents you are asked to complete.

If that happens, nothing about your method changes. You are still recording the same items in the same way. It is worth matching the client's vocabulary in the paperwork you send back, simply because it avoids a pointless round of "is this the same thing you asked for?".

## Related terms that are not synonyms

A few terms sit nearby and are genuinely different, and mixing them up causes real confusion.

**Defects list.** Often used interchangeably with snag list, but on some contracts "defects" specifically means items raised during the defects liability period after handover, rather than items found at handover itself. Read the contract before assuming.

**Practical completion.** Not a list at all. It is the point at which the works are complete enough for the client to take possession, usually with a snag list attached. See [practical completion](/glossary/practical-completion/) in the glossary.

**Handover.** The event of giving the building over, of which snagging is one part.

## The label matters less than the format

Whichever word your client uses, a list only works if each entry can be acted on without a phone call. That means:

1. A photograph showing the defect clearly.
2. A location precise enough to walk to.
3. A short description of what is wrong.
4. A number, so items can be referenced and ticked off.

A "punch list" of forty vague lines is worse than a "snag list" of forty photographed, located items, and vice versa. The vocabulary is cosmetic. The structure is what gets things fixed.
