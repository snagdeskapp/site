Plenty of good site managers run their snagging on a spreadsheet, and for some jobs that is a perfectly sensible choice. It is worth being honest about where the line is rather than pretending a spreadsheet is useless.

## Where a spreadsheet holds up

If you are logging a handful of items, you already have the file open, and nobody is expecting photographs, a spreadsheet is quick and free. It sorts, it filters, and everyone can open it. For a small internal list that never leaves your own team, that is often enough.

It also has one real advantage: it is completely flexible. You can add any column you like.

## Where it starts to cost you

The trouble is photographs, and photographs are most of the value in snagging.

**Linking photos to rows is manual.** You take pictures on your phone, then later you have to work out which of ninety images belongs to which row, rename them, and either embed them or send them alongside. This is the step that turns a one-hour walk into a three-hour evening.

**Markup is a separate job.** Drawing a box around the defect means opening an image editor, editing, saving, re-inserting. Most people skip it, which is why so many snag lists get disputed.

**Capture on site is slow.** Typing into a spreadsheet on a phone, standing in a half-finished room with one hand free, is unpleasant enough that items get "remembered for later". Some never make it in.

**The output does not look like a report.** A spreadsheet emailed to a client reads as working notes. It is harder to hand to a homeowner or a developer as a formal record.

**Templates drift.** Everyone ends up with a slightly different version of the file, and the format changes job to job.

## Where an app wins

A purpose-built snagging app collapses the whole sequence into one step. You photograph the defect, mark it up on the image, add the location and note, and it is filed against the right job. When the walk finishes, the report generates itself with every snag numbered, located and photographed.

That is the actual difference: not features, but the removal of the reconciliation work between photos and rows.

## An honest comparison

| | Spreadsheet | Snagging app |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | Free | Free or one-time purchase |
| Photos linked to items | Manual | Automatic |
| Markup on the photo | Separate editing | Built in |
| Capture speed on site | Slow on a phone | Designed for it |
| Client-ready report | Needs formatting | One tap |
| Flexibility of columns | Total | Fixed structure |

## How to choose

Ask two questions.

**How many snags do you log in a typical week?** Below a handful, the spreadsheet overhead is small. Above that, the photo reconciliation starts eating real time.

**Does the list leave your team?** If a client, homeowner or developer receives it, presentation matters and a spreadsheet undersells the work you did.
