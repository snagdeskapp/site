Snagging is the process of going through a finished or nearly finished building and recording everything that has not been built, installed or finished to the expected standard. Each item recorded is a **snag**. The collected items are a **snag list**, and the document you hand over is usually called a snagging report.

The word is used across UK construction, from a single house extension to a block of flats. It is not a complaint process and it is not about being difficult. It is the normal, expected step that closes the gap between "the works are finished" and "the works are actually right".

## What counts as a snag

A snag is anything that falls short of what was specified or what a reasonable standard would be. In practice most snags are small and cosmetic, and a smaller number are functional.

- **Finish defects:** paint coverage, visible joints, uneven plaster, marks and scratches.
- **Fit and alignment:** doors catching on frames, cupboard doors out of line, tiles out of level.
- **Sealing and waterproofing:** gaps in silicone beads, missing mastic, poorly finished joints.
- **Services:** sockets sitting proud or crooked, switches not level, alarms not flush to the ceiling.
- **Damage:** chips, cracks and scuffs caused during the works.
- **Incomplete work:** something simply not done, such as a missing trim or an unfitted vent.

What is normally not a snag is a design decision you dislike, or ordinary wear from use after you move in. Snagging records the state of the building at handover, not preferences.

## Who does the snagging

It depends on the job.

On a **new build purchase**, the buyer often snags the property themselves before or shortly after legal completion, and some hire a professional snagging inspector to do a formal inspection and produce a report.

On a **contracted build or refurbishment**, the contractor snags their own work before offering it for handover, and the client or their surveyor carries out their own inspection. Site managers also run snagging as work completes plot by plot, so items get closed out during the build rather than piling up at the end.

## When snagging happens

The most useful moment is **just before or at handover**, once the property is complete but before it is furnished. An empty property is much easier to inspect: skirtings, floors and walls are all visible, and nothing needs moving.

Many developers also operate a **defects period** after completion, commonly of a set length agreed in the contract, during which items reported are still put right. Finding something later is not fatal, but the earlier it is recorded the stronger the position.

## What makes a snag list work

The difference between a snag list that gets actioned and one that gets argued about is specificity. Three things matter:

1. **A photograph that clearly shows the defect.** If the person fixing it cannot see the problem in the picture, the entry is weak.
2. **A location precise enough to find it.** "Bedroom 2, window cill, left return" beats "upstairs".
3. **A short, factual description.** What is wrong, not how annoyed you are about it.

Marking up the photograph, by drawing a box or a circle on the image, removes almost all of the ambiguity. It is the single biggest improvement most people can make to their snag lists.

## From list to report

The list only creates value when it reaches the builder or developer in a form they can work from. A numbered, photographed, located report gets actioned far more reliably than a text message or a verbal walk-round. That is the whole point of the exercise: not to catalogue faults, but to get them fixed.
